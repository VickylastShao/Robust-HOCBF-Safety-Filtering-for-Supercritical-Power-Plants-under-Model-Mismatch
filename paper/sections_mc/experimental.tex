\section{Experimental Validation}
\label{sec:exp}

The experiments are designed to evaluate whether the proposed filter can operate as a real-time safety layer in an industrial measurement and control loop. Therefore, we report not only CBF violation rates but also tracking IAE, input variation, QP intervention rate, inter-sample behavior, and per-step computational latency---metrics that characterize the filter's suitability for commissioning in an industrial DCS environment.

\subsection{Benchmark and Baselines}

The benchmark is a 5th-order nonlinear model of a 1000\,MW ultra-supercritical boiler-turbine unit with state $x = [r_B, p_m, h_m, N_e, \tau_f]^\top$ and three control inputs. Six safety constraints are enforced: main steam pressure ($m=2$, 13--24\,MPa), separator enthalpy ($m=1$, 2670--2830\,kJ/kg), and power output ($m=1$, $\pm50$\,MW). The control effectiveness is scaled by the state-dependent function $\Phi(p_m)$ during evaluation while the HOCBF uses the linearized model---a realistic stress test of safety filter robustness.

Six static perturbation scenarios model realistic disturbances: S1 (coal quality drift, $-15\%$ heating value), S2 (feedwater pump cycling), S3 (coupled state-dependent, $\delta \propto x$), S4 (nonlinear fouling), S5 (valve degradation, modelled as equivalent drift $\Deltaf$ via reduced steam extraction efficiency; the $\Delta g = 0$ assumption is maintained throughout all scenarios), and S6 (fuel quality variation). Scenarios S1--S4 are common to the 3rd- and 5th-order CCS models; S5 and S6 are specific to the 5th-order model, affecting the extended states $N_e$ and $\tau_f$.

Methods compared: PPO (no safety layer), PPO-Lagrangian (dual-descent constraint penalty), PPO-CBF (first-order CBF, $m{=}1$ for all constraints---deliberate ablation), PPO-HOCBF (correct relative degrees $m{=}2,1,1$, no GP), PPO-GP-HOCBF (GP mean correction, $\epsilon_\kappa{=}0$), PPO-RHOCBF (scenario-specific GP + compositional $\epsilon(x)$, $\epsilon_\kappa{=}1.0$), RoCBF-Net (online GP adaptation, $\epsilon_\kappa{=}1.0$), and NMPC (SLSQP solver, $N{=}5$ horizon). GP pretraining: $N=500$ points per scenario (5th-order CCS, $\Phi$-scaled rollout). Evaluation: 5 seeds $\times$ 50 episodes $\times$ 500 steps.

Table~\ref{tab:safety} reports the main results. Five key findings emerge. \textbf{(1) Safety layer alone fails under uncertainty.} PPO without safety, PPO-Lagrangian (dual descent), and PPO-CBF (first-order, $m{=}1$ for all constraints) all exceed 99\% violation under any dynamics-affecting scenario---soft constraints and incorrect relative degree offer no protection. PPO-HOCBF with correct relative degrees ($m{=}2,1,1$) but no GP achieves 0.00\% on nominal but still $>$99\% under uncertainty: correct mathematical structure is necessary but insufficient without uncertainty modeling. \textbf{(2) GP mean correction is the dominant safety mechanism.} PPO-GP-HOCBF ($\epsilon_\kappa{=}0$, GP mean correction only) reduces violation to $\leq$0.18\% under six of seven uncertainty scenarios---a $>$500$\times$ improvement over PPO-HOCBF---without any robustness margin. This establishes GP residual modeling, not the compositional $\epsilon(x)$ margin, as the primary driver of empirical safety. The exception is S3 (Coupled, state-dependent), where GP mean correction alone leaves 39.65\% violation; the state-dependent feedback amplification escapes pure mean-level correction.
\textbf{(3) $\epsilon_\kappa{=}1.0$ (theoretical bound) is overly conservative.} PPO-RHOCBF with $\epsilon_\kappa{=}1.0$ degrades on S2 (99.43\%) and S4 (32.19\%) compared to $\epsilon_\kappa{=}0$ (0.04\% and 0.04\%, respectively). The full theoretical margin overly constrains the QP feasible set, forcing constraint-dropping fallback and thereby increasing violations rather than preventing them. RoCBF-Net with online GP adaptation shares this conservatism, underperforming the static GP variant on most uncertainty conditions. An $\epsilon_\kappa$ sensitivity sweep (Sec.~\ref{sec:kappa}) confirms that the optimal scaling lies in $[0,0.3]$ for additive uncertainty and $[0.1,0.5]$ for state-dependent uncertainty---far below the theoretical $\kappa{=}1.0$.
\textbf{(4) NMPC is the optimal benchmark.} NMPC with $N{=}5$ horizon achieves $\leq$0.08\% violation across all conditions, establishing an upper performance bound. Its per-step cost (${\sim}254$\,ms, CPU) is approximately $10\times$ higher than the CBF-QP filter (${\sim}25$\,ms, GPU), a trade-off discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:comp}.
\textbf{(5) Violations are enthalpy-dominated.} The per-constraint logs reveal zero observed violations on pressure ($m{=}2$) and power ($m{=}1$) constraints; the aggregate violations arise from the enthalpy constraint ($m{=}1$). This bottleneck reflects both its tighter safety bounds (2670--2830\,kJ/kg) and the direct impact of heat-absorption uncertainty on the enthalpy state.
These results reveal a clear hierarchy: correct HOCBF structure is necessary, GP uncertainty correction is the primary safety mechanism, and $\epsilon_\kappa$ provides a tunable safety--performance trade-off best calibrated per uncertainty type rather than fixed at the worst-case theoretical bound.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$\epsilon_\kappa$}{epsilon-kappa} Sensitivity: One Safety Factor Does Not Fit All}
\label{sec:kappa}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/kappa_sensitivity.pdf}
    \caption{$\epsilon_\kappa$ sensitivity sweep across three representative uncertainty scenarios (5th-order CCS). Optimal $\kappa$ differs by uncertainty type: additive uncertainty (S2, S4) achieves zero violation at $\kappa{=}0$ and collapses at $\kappa{\geq}0.5$; state-dependent uncertainty (S3) requires $\kappa{>}0$ (fails at $\kappa{=}0$ with 33.5\% violation) but remains safe up to $\kappa{=}0.5$ (0.25\%). $\kappa{=}1.0$ is never the optimal choice on any condition.}
    \label{fig:kappa_sweep}
\end{figure}

To quantify the practical safety--performance trade-off, we swept $\epsilon_\kappa \in \{0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0\}$ across three representative conditions (2--3 seeds per setting, 5th-order CCS): S2 (additive oscillation), S3 (state-dependent coupled), and S4 (nonlinear fouling). Figure~\ref{fig:kappa_sweep} reports the results.

\textbf{Additive uncertainty (S2, S4):} $\kappa{=}0$ achieves zero violation. The GP mean correction alone compensates for bounded, non-amplifying perturbations. Increasing $\kappa$ to $0.5$ collapses QP feasibility on S2 (99.4\% violation) while S4 remains safe (0.10\%)---the nonlinear fouling perturbation structure preserves a larger feasible set than the pressure oscillation. $\kappa^*_{\mathrm{S2}}{=}0$, $\kappa^*_{\mathrm{S4}}{=}0$.

\textbf{State-dependent uncertainty (S3):} $\kappa{=}0$ fails (33.5\% violation)---GP mean correction cannot track the feedback-amplified perturbation. Adding even a small margin ($\kappa{=}0.1$) restores zero-violation safety, and safety is maintained through $\kappa{=}0.5$ (0.25\%). $\kappa{=}1.0$ collapses (40.3\%). $\kappa^*_{\mathrm{S3}}{=}0.1$.

\textbf{Practical recommendations:} For additive uncertainty ($|\delta|$ bounded), $\epsilon_\kappa \in [0, 0.3]$ suffices; GP mean correction is the primary mechanism. For state-dependent uncertainty ($\delta \propto x$), $\epsilon_\kappa \in [0.1, 0.5]$ is recommended---the margin is necessary but should not be maximal. $\epsilon_\kappa{=}1.0$ (the theoretical worst-case bound) is never optimal in practice. These findings motivate treating $\epsilon_\kappa$ as a tunable safety factor calibrated per uncertainty type rather than a fixed theoretical constant.


\subsection{Deployment Envelope: \texorpdfstring{$\epsilon_\kappa$}{epsilon-kappa} Under Varying Coupling Strength}
\label{sec:envelope}

Section~\ref{sec:kappa} established that the optimal $\epsilon_\kappa$ differs between additive and state-dependent uncertainty. A natural follow-on question is whether the \emph{strength} of state-dependent coupling also matters---and if so, whether the safe $\epsilon_\kappa$ range shrinks as coupling intensifies. To answer this, we swept $\epsilon_\kappa \in \{0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0\}$ across four S3 coupling strengths, scaling the state-dependent feedback gain $\gamma \in \{0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ in the perturbation function $\delta(x) = [0, \gamma\,c_p (x_1 - x_1^0) - d_p, 0.5\gamma\,c_h (x_2 - x_2^0) - d_h, 0, 0]^\top$ (2 seeds $\times$ 50 episodes $\times$ 500 steps, 5th-order CCS). Figure~\ref{fig:kappa_gradient} reports the results.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/kappa_s3_gradient.pdf}
    \caption{Deployment envelope of $\epsilon_\kappa$ under varying S3 coupling strength $\gamma$ (5th-order CCS). \textbf{Left:} violation rate vs.\ $\epsilon_\kappa$ for four coupling strengths. \textbf{Right:} safe $\epsilon_\kappa$ range (defined as $<$1\% violation) as a function of $\gamma$. At weak coupling ($\gamma{=}0.5$), $\kappa{=}0$ already fails (8.3\%) and a small margin ($\kappa{=}0.1$) restores zero violation. At medium coupling ($\gamma{=}1.0$), the $\kappa{=}0$ failure deepens (33.5\%) while $\kappa{=}0.1$ maintains safety. At mid-strong coupling ($\gamma{=}1.5$), the reversal is already complete: $\kappa{=}0$ achieves 0.51\% violation while any $\kappa{>}0$ collapses QP feasibility (75.3\%--97.3\%). The envelope closes between $\gamma{=}1.0$ and $\gamma{=}1.5$. At strong coupling ($\gamma{=}2.0$), any $\kappa{>}0$ collapses QP feasibility ($>$98\% violation) while $\kappa{=}0$ achieves near-zero violation (0.19\%)---the perturbation grows faster than the CBF Lie-derivative chain can compensate, so adding margin only constrains the QP without providing protection. The safe $\epsilon_\kappa$ envelope closes between $\gamma{=}1.0$ and $\gamma{=}1.5$.}
    \label{fig:kappa_gradient}
\end{figure}

Three regimes emerge from the coupling gradient. \textbf{(i) GP-sufficient regime ($\gamma < 0.5$):} GP mean correction alone provides near-perfect safety---the state-dependent perturbation is weak enough that the GP prediction tracks it without a robustness margin. \textbf{(ii) $\epsilon_\kappa$-useful regime ($0.5 \leq \gamma < 1.5$):} the perturbation outgrows the GP correction at $\kappa{=}0$, and a small positive margin ($\kappa \in [0.1, 0.3]$) restores zero-violation safety. Within this regime, the safe $\kappa$ range narrows as $\gamma$ increases: $\kappa \in [0.1, 1.0]$ at $\gamma{=}0.5$ but shrinks to $\kappa \in [0.1, 0.5]$ at $\gamma{=}1.0$. \textbf{(iii) Deployment boundary ($\gamma \geq 1.5$):} the perturbation amplification rate exceeds the CBF Lie-derivative chain's compensation capacity. Adding $\epsilon_\kappa$ is counterproductive---it constrains the QP without addressing the fundamental limitation that the HOCBF structure cannot certify safety under this perturbation growth rate. $\kappa{=}0$ remains marginally safe (0.51\% at $\gamma{=}1.5$, 0.19\% at $\gamma{=}2.0$) but any positive $\kappa$ collapses feasibility (75--98\% at $\gamma{=}1.5$, 98--100\% at $\gamma{=}2.0$). The filter remains useful as a diagnostic: QP infeasibility at high coupling signals that the system should fall back to a conservative schedule or raise an alarm.

\textbf{Implication.} The coupling gradient defines a deployment envelope for $\epsilon_\kappa$ (Table~\ref{tab:deployment_envelope}). Rather than fixing $\kappa$ to a theoretical worst-case value (1.0), commissioning involves characterizing the expected coupling strength and selecting $\kappa$ from the appropriate regime. For industrial CCS units operating under known state-dependent coupling profiles (e.g.,$\,$S3 with $\gamma \approx 1.0$), $\kappa = 0.1$ provides the safety--performance optimum. For units entering service with unknown perturbation characteristics, a conservative initial setting of $\kappa = 0.3$ balances safety against excessive QP intervention, with the option to reduce $\kappa$ toward $0$ as operational data confirms GP model adequacy. The deployment boundary at $\gamma \geq 1.5$ reveals a fundamental limitation: when the perturbation amplifies faster than the Lie-derivative chain can compensate, no $\epsilon_\kappa$ value helps, and the QP infeasibility signal should trigger a fallback rather than a margin increase.


\subsection{Filter Diagnostics and Computational Performance}
\label{sec:comp}

Figure~\ref{fig:traj} compares HOCBF (no GP) and GP-HOCBF ($\epsilon_\kappa{=}0$) under S3: the nominal filter allows enthalpy to drop below its lower bound on every step, while the GP-corrected filter substantially reduces violations (1.3\% vs.\ 100\% on the shown trajectory). Under state-dependent coupling, GP mean correction alone leaves residual violations (39.65\% aggregate, Table~\ref{tab:safety}) because the feedback-amplified perturbation escapes pure mean-level correction---motivating a small positive $\epsilon_\kappa$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:kappa}). Figure~\ref{fig:epsilon} quantifies the compositional $\epsilon(x)$ margin under S3, showing that $\epsilon_h$ varies by more than one order of magnitude across the operating enthalpy range (CV 105\%). This state dependence is pronounced only under state-dependent perturbations (S3); under constant perturbations (S1, S2, S4--S6), $\epsilon(x)$ is approximately constant and dominated by the GP regularization floor---consistent with the $\epsilon_\kappa$ sweep finding that additive uncertainty requires negligible margin. Figure~\ref{fig:qp} shows the violation rate as a function of perturbation magnitude under GP-HOCBF ($\epsilon_\kappa{=}0$). The filter maintains zero violation up to Mag50 (50\,kJ/kg enthalpy perturbation), beyond which (Mag75, 75\,kJ/kg) the perturbation outgrows the GP correction capacity and violations rise to 100\%. This defines a sharp safe operating envelope for the GP-only configuration.

Per-step computation: the safety-filter pipeline (PPO+GP+QP) with GPU JIT compilation requires ${\sim}25$\,ms (p95: 27\,ms), including the PPO forward pass, GP inference, and QP solve; NMPC (CPU, scipy SLSQP) averages 254\,ms. The filter provides per-step rather than horizon-based safety evaluation, approximately ten times faster than the implemented SLSQP-based baseline. For CPU-only deployment, the distilled policy achieves 1.8\,ms inference (empirical-only safety, without the certified robustness margin). The reported comparison is made against the implemented SLSQP-based NMPC baseline rather than optimized industrial NMPC implementations.

The GP-HOCBF filter operates as a minimally invasive safety overlay: when the RL policy outputs actions that satisfy all CBF constraints, the QP returns the action unchanged (zero intervention). Under $\epsilon_\kappa{=}0$, the QP rarely intervenes on nominal and additive-uncertainty conditions (S1, S2, S4--S6, Load Following), acting only when the GP residual prediction indicates an impending constraint boundary crossing. The filter is thus \textbf{minimally invasive under low uncertainty}. Under S3 (state-dependent), where the GP alone is insufficient, a modest margin ($\epsilon_\kappa{=}0.1$) restores the safety guarantee at negligible control cost. Under $\epsilon_\kappa{=}1.0$, the filter intervenes on nearly every step---the QP is over-constrained and must frequently relax constraints via the weak-authority fallback, negating the safety benefit of the added margin. This confirms that $\epsilon_\kappa$ controls the \textbf{intervention--safety trade-off}: zero for minimal invasiveness on well-modeled systems, a small positive value for state-dependent uncertainty, and never the full theoretical bound.

\subsection{Deployment Considerations}

The method is intended as a \textbf{commissioning-calibrated safety filter} with a tunable robustness factor $\epsilon_\kappa$. Deployment involves two independent decisions: how to train the GP, and how to set $\epsilon_\kappa$.

\textbf{GP calibration strategy.} Three modes define a clear deployment envelope:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Scenario-specific GP} (recommended): calibrated on commissioning data from the target operating condition. Provides the tightest residual model and enables the lowest practical $\epsilon_\kappa$.
    \item \textbf{Online GP adaptation}: incrementally updated during operation. Enables deployment without prior scenario knowledge, at the cost of a transient period without full GP accuracy.
    \item \textbf{Generic mixed GP}: trained on pooled data from multiple scenarios without scenario labeling. Defined as outside the deployment envelope---silent calibration failure produces 82.7--99.7\% violation (Phase~4, 3rd-order).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{$\epsilon_\kappa$ selection.} Once the GP is calibrated, $\epsilon_\kappa$ should be set according to the expected uncertainty structure and strength (Sec.~\ref{sec:envelope}):
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Additive uncertainty} ($|\delta|$ bounded, scenarios S1, S2, S4--S6): $\epsilon_\kappa \in [0, 0.3]$. GP mean correction alone provides sufficient safety; the margin can be set to zero for minimal control intervention.
    \item \textbf{State-dependent uncertainty in the $\epsilon_\kappa$-useful regime} ($0.5 \leq \gamma < 1.5$, scenario S3): $\epsilon_\kappa \in [0.1, 0.3]$. A positive margin is necessary---GP mean correction alone fails at $\gamma \geq 0.5$ ($>$8\% violation). As coupling strengthens, the safe $\kappa$ range narrows but the optimum remains within this narrow band; $\epsilon_\kappa{=}1.0$ always over-constrains the QP.
    \item \textbf{Strong state-dependent uncertainty beyond the envelope} ($\gamma \geq 1.5$, scenario S3): the HOCBF Lie-derivative chain cannot compensate for the perturbation amplification rate. Adding $\epsilon_\kappa$ is counterproductive (75--99\% violation at any $\kappa{>}0$). The QP infeasibility diagnostic should trigger a fallback to a conservative operating regime rather than increasing $\kappa$.
    \item \textbf{No uncertainty} (nominal, load following): $\epsilon_\kappa = 0$, and the GP can be omitted entirely (pure HOCBF suffices).
\end{enumerate}

Prior cross-scenario validation (Phase~4, 3rd-order) confirmed the out-of-distribution failure mode (S3$\to$S1: 99.9\% violation). This failure mode is explicitly characterized, not hidden: $\sigmaGP$ measures spatial distance to training data, not functional distance between perturbation structures. The $\Delta g=0$ assumption limits applicability to systems where drift uncertainty dominates. QP infeasibility serves as a one-sided diagnostic: it detects overestimated $\sigmaGP$ (overly conservative margin) but not underestimated $\sigmaGP$ (unsafe failure). The practical deployment recommendation is therefore: calibrate the GP on scenario-representative data (mode i), then select $\epsilon_\kappa$ from the regime schedule above, bounded by the coupling-strength deployment envelope established in Sec.~\ref{sec:envelope}.
