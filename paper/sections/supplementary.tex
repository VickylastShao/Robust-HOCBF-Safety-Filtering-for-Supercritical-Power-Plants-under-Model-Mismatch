\section{Systematic Inter-Sample Safety Analysis}

The CBF safety guarantee is a continuous-time certificate evaluated at discrete sampling instants. Zero-order-hold (ZOH) implementation with $T_s = 1$\,s means the safety filter computes a constraint-satisfying control $u_{\mathrm{safe}}$ at time $t_k$ and holds it constant over $[t_k, t_{k+1})$. Between samples, the true continuous-time trajectory may temporarily exit the safe set before the next filter intervention. Table~\ref{tab:S8_inter_sample} reports a systematic inter-sample safety analysis across all seven deployment conditions (Nominal + S1--S6), using 4 sub-step linear interpolation per ZOH interval to approximate the continuous-time constraint evolution.

These results reveal a structural limitation of the LQR-only baseline under 5th-order dynamics: the upstream LQR controller ($v_{\mathrm{RL}} = 0$) provides no anticipatory action to avoid constraint boundaries, and the safety filter alone cannot recover from persistent state-dependent perturbations (S1--S3) or 5th-order-specific extended-state perturbations (S5--S6) with $N_{\mathrm{GP}}=500$ scenario-specific pretraining. Only the Nominal (unperturbed) and S4: Nonlinear Fouling scenarios achieve zero inter-sample violations---the latter because the quadratic pressure-dependence perturbation structure does not drive the system toward the constrained boundaries under the $\Phi$-scaled rollout. The maximum excursion under the compositional $\epsilon(x)$ is reduced compared to the flat-UCB ablation (S1: $17.2\pm7.0$ vs.\ $19.8\pm4.5$; S3: $17.2\pm2.3$ vs.\ $18.8\pm0.4$; S5: $2.6\pm0.0$ vs.\ $2.2\pm0.0$), confirming that the per-level $\sigma$-chain propagation provides finer-grained robustness allocation than a single constant margin, but the compositional margin alone is insufficient to guarantee forward invariance when the upstream controller does not actively avoid constraint boundaries. The inter-sample violation rate mirrors the discrete-time CBF violation rate across all scenarios, indicating that inter-sample excursions are not an independent failure mode---they occur when the CBF constraint itself is violated at sampling instants. Achieving zero inter-sample violations on the 5th-order benchmark requires either (a) a trained PPO actor that learns boundary-avoiding trajectories (as demonstrated in Phase~4 on 3rd-order dynamics) or (b) larger GP pretraining datasets ($N > 500$) to reduce the compositional $\epsilon(x)$ margin further. These results are reported on the 5th-order dynamics ($x \in \mathbb{R}^5$) while the Phase~4 inter-method comparison (Table~1, main text) was conducted on the 3rd-order dynamics ($x \in \mathbb{R}^3$); the generalization of the safety guarantee across model orders is an open question.

\section{GP Calibration Diagnostics}

\section{Flat-GP-UCB Ablation: Compositional vs.\ Constant Robustness Margin}

The compositional $\sigma$-chain (Eqs.~7--9 in the main manuscript) propagates per-dimension GP uncertainty through successive $\psi$-chain levels, producing a state-dependent robustness margin $\epsilon(x)$. A natural ablation is to replace $\epsilon(x)$ with a \emph{constant} GP-UCB margin $\epsilon_0 = \beta\cdot\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{GP}}$ calibrated from the mean GP uncertainty over the operating region---i.e., applying GP-UCB flatly at the final QP constraint level without per-level propagation. This ablation isolates the contribution of compositional margin propagation: if the flat-UCB baseline achieves comparable safety, the compositional $\sigma$-chain is unnecessary; if it degrades, the per-level propagation is structurally needed.

Table~\ref{tab:S10_flat_ucb} reports the comparison between three GP-UCB margin strategies under 5th-order $\Phi$-scaled nonlinear dynamics with $N_{\mathrm{GP}}=500$ scenario-specific pretraining:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \textbf{PPO-GP-HOCBF}: GP mean correction only, $\epsilon(x) = 0$ (no uncertainty margin);
    \item \textbf{Flat-GP-UCB} (this ablation): GP mean correction + constant $\epsilon_0$ calibrated as the 90th percentile of $\beta\cdot\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{GP}}$ over 200 sampled operating states;
    \item \textbf{PPO-RHOCBF} (ours): GP mean correction + compositional $\epsilon(x)$ from the recursive $\sigma$-chain (Eqs.~7--9, $\epsilonKappa=1.0$).
\end{enumerate}
All three variants use the same scenario-specific GP; they differ only in how---and whether---the GP posterior uncertainty $\sigma_{\mathrm{GP}}(x)$ is propagated into the robustness margin.

The results demonstrate three failure modes of the constant GP-UCB margin relative to the compositional $\epsilon(x)$.

\textbf{(1) Over-conservatism under structured perturbations (S1, S3).} Under S1 (Heat) and S3 (Coupled), the constant $\epsilon_0$ must be calibrated at the 90th percentile of $\beta\cdot\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{GP}}$ over the operating region to cover high-uncertainty states, producing $\epsilon_0 = 21.7$ and $\epsilon_0 = 65.6$ respectively. These margins are 1--2 orders of magnitude larger than the typical compositional $\epsilon(x)$ near equilibrium (cf.\ Table~S3), making the QP constraint $A u \leq b - \epsilon_0$ excessively tight. The QP intervention rate saturates at 83\% (S1) and 82\% (S3), yet the constant margin still fails to prevent 17--18\% CBF violations because $\epsilon_0$ is simultaneously too large at low-uncertainty states (causing QP infeasibility and fallback to the LQR-stabilized baseline) and too small at high-uncertainty states (where the true $\epsilon(x)$ would be larger than $\epsilon_0$). The high inter-seed variance ($\pm 23\%$) further indicates that the constant margin lacks the adaptivity needed for reliable safety across random seeds.

\textbf{(2) Silent failure under extended-state perturbations (S5).} Under S5 (Valve degradation, 5th-order dynamics), $\epsilon_0 = 0.539$ is nearly identical to the Nominal value ($0.540$), yet the CBF violation rate reaches 99.5\%. The GP residual model captures $\Delta f$ only in the 3D core states $(r_B, p_m, h_m)$, but the valve degradation perturbs the extended-state dynamics $(N_e, \tau_f)$ through the full 5D coupling. The GP's $\sigma_{\mathrm{GP}}(x)$ at sampled 3D states appears benign, leading to a small $\epsilon_0$ that provides essentially no robustness margin. The compositional $\epsilon(x)$, by contrast, propagates uncertainty through the $\psi$-chain using the full 5D dynamics (via $L_f$ and $L_g$ operators), so even small 3D GP uncertainty is amplified by the extended-state coupling captured in the Lie derivatives. This explains why PPO-RHOCBF achieves 0\% violation under S5 while the flat-UCB baseline fails completely---the per-level $\sigma$-chain sees what the scalar $\epsilon_0$ cannot.

\textbf{(3) Structural necessity of per-level propagation.} The constant $\epsilon_0$ aggregates all GP uncertainty into a single scalar subtracted from the final QP RHS, corresponding to a flat $\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}$ without the recursive coupling weights $c_j = \prod_{i=j+1}^{m-1} (\|L_{\hat{f}}\|_{\mathrm{op}} + k_i)$. Under S3 (Coupled), where the perturbation structure produces state-dependent uncertainty amplification across relative-degree levels, the coupling weights $c_1 = \|L_{\hat{f}}\|_{\mathrm{op}} + k_1 \approx 3.5$ amplify the first-level $\sigma_1$ by a factor of 3--4$\times$ in the total margin (see Eq.~7--9, main manuscript, and Lemma~S1 in Appendix~S\ref{sec:appendix_proofs}). The flat-UCB baseline cannot capture this amplification, treating all GP uncertainty as if it enters at the final constraint level. The 18.2\% violation rate under S3 confirms that per-level propagation is structurally necessary when the perturbation structure couples across HOCBF relative-degree levels.
